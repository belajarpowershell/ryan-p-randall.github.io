Evaluating appropriate secondary sources

Secondary sources are:

  • removed from the event by degrees of time or involvement
  • often summarizing or analyzing
  • often journalistic or historical
Let's think about secondary sources for a moment. What are they, and why would we want to use them for an ethics analysis paper? Can anyone remind me of what makes a secondary source different from a primary source? Do they have a different type of authority than primary sources? They're separated from the "primary" event, either by time, or participation, or both. They often do the work of summary or analysis. They're also often written with journalistic or historical tone.

Evaluating with "CAARP" test

  • Currency, or when—timeliness of information
  • Authority, or who—expertise of author
  • Accuracy, or how—support for claims with references
  • Relevance, or why for your essay—suitability of approach and language
  • Purpose, or why written at all—original reason for publication

Sarah Blakeslee, a librarian at California State University, Chico introduced this rubric with the acronymn "CRAAP," but it's memorable in any order. The "timeliness" of information can vary depending on what type of information you're discussing. For instance, Blakeslee wrote about this acronym in 2004, but the principles she discusses are still timely. But can you think of something written in 2004 that would almost certainly be outdated by now? Just as a random technology example, iPhones weren't introuced until 2007!

Evaluating appropriate secondary sources

You'll want to keep track of:

  • Author's Name
  • Title of author's work (the whole title!)
  • Publication's Title
  • Publication's Location
  • Publication Date
  • Date you accessed any online materials (they can disappear!)
  • Page numbers, URLs, or DOIs (DOIs are basically fancier URLs)
