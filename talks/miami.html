Evaluating appropriate secondary sources

Secondary sources are:

  • removed from the event by degrees of time or involvement
  • often summarizing or analyzing
  • often journalistic or historical
Let's think about secondary sources for a moment. What are they, and why would we want to use them for an ethics analysis paper? Can anyone remind me of what makes a secondary source different from a primary source? Do they have a different type of authority than primary sources?

Evaluating with "CAARP" test

  • Currency, or when—timeliness of information
  • Authority, or who—expertise of author
  • Accuracy, or how—support for claims with references
  • Relevance, or why for your essay—suitability of approach and language
  • Purpose, or why written at all—original reason for publication

Sarah Blakeslee, a librarian at California State University, Chico introduced this rubric with the acronymn "CRAAP," but it's memorable in any order. The "timeliness" of information can vary depending on what type of information you're discussing.
